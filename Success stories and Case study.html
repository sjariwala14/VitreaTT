SingHealth Cybersecurity Modernization

SingHealth initiated a strategic project to eliminate critical vulnerabilities in its advanced visualization platform, resulting in a 100% elimination of RDP exposure and 40% increase in clinician satisfaction.

  • Challenge
  • Solution
  • Results
  • Implementation

Background & Challenge

In response to a 300% surge in ransomware attacks targeting healthcare imaging systems, SingHealth needed to eliminate reliance on Remote Desktop Protocol (RDP) as the primary entry point for its advanced visualization platform.

The challenge was to architect a solution that adhered to stringent Zero Trust (NIST SP 800-207) and accessibility (WCAG 2.1) standards without disrupting clinical workflows across its three-hospital network.

Solution: A Re-architected, Zero-Trust Platform

The project involved a phased transition from a legacy, RDP-dependent system to a modernized, HTML5 zero-footprint architecture with meticulously segmented infrastructure:

  • Elimination of RDP with all client delivery through encrypted HTTPS/TLS 1.3 sessions
  • Segmented architecture with core components on separate VLANs
  • Separation of management functions into dedicated servers
  • Secure integration with existing systems
  • Centralized monitoring and logging solution

Measurable Results & Outcomes

The modernization yielded significant, quantifiable improvements:

  • 100% Elimination of RDP Exposure
  • 40% increase in clinician satisfaction scores
  • 60% reduction in resistance to rollout
  • Zero critical vulnerabilities over 12-month monitoring period

Phased Implementation

The deployment was executed in a carefully planned phased manner:

  1. Phase 1: SGH Campus (including the National Heart Centre and National Cancer Centre)
  2. Phase 2: Changi General Hospital (CGH)
  3. Phase 3: KK Women's and Children's Hospital (KKH)

IHH Pantai Advanced Visualization Solution

Deployment of an advanced visualization and post-processing solution across the IHH Healthcare network in Malaysia (16 hospitals) meeting specific clinical functionality requirements while ensuring optimal performance, scalability, and integration.

  • Objective
  • Requirements
  • Architecture
  • Recommendation

Objective

To deploy an advanced visualization (AV) and post-processing solution across the IHH Healthcare network in Malaysia (16 hospitals) that meets specific clinical functionality requirements while ensuring optimal performance, scalability, and integration within the existing IT infrastructure.

Key Functional Requirements

The solution must provide a PACS Imaging Client/Workstation capable of:

  • Advanced 3D Post-Processing: Including Liver, Vessel, and Lung Analysis, Virtual CT Colonography, Dual Energy, and CT/MR Perfusion
  • Seamless Integration: Ability to run on standard Windows PCs, support multiple monitors, and integrate with third-party AI and clinical applications
  • Comprehensive Data Support: Handling complex imaging data such as multi-frame angio, cine Doppler ultrasound, and video fluoroscopy
  • Efficient Workflow: Compliance with IHE TCE profile, smart pre-fetching of studies based on procedure and user worklists

Proposed Architectural Options

Four deployment models were evaluated to balance performance, centralization, and cost for the 16 hospitals:

Option Architecture Pros Cons
1: Centralized DB & License Single Management Server in a central Data Centre Centralized database and license management High network dependency, potential for image lag
2: Decentralized (ESS) Enterprise Single Server deployed at each local site Best performance with data processed locally Decentralized database, limited storage per server
3: Hybrid Decentralized Full Management + Application Server stack at each site Excellent performance for its group, flexible licensing Complex setup, decentralized management
4 (Recommended): Flexible Hybrid Management Server + ESS at each local Data Centre Ideal balance of performance without central latency Decentralized database requires robust PACS integration

Recommendation

Option 4 (Flexible Hybrid) is presented as the strongest candidate. It effectively addresses the performance limitations of a fully centralized model and the scalability limitations of a purely per-site model, while offering more manageable complexity than Option 3.

It ensures fast image delivery for radiologists by processing data within their geographic region while maintaining the flexibility for centralized license management or allocation to specific hospital groups.

Nuevo Hospital Militar Redundancy Solution

Implementation of a high-availability, disaster-resistant architecture for the Canon Vitrea Enterprise advanced visualization system at Nuevo Hospital Militar Managua to ensure business continuity with minimal downtime and data loss.

  • Objective
  • Current State
  • Solution
  • Recommendation

Objective

To design a high-availability, disaster-resistant architecture for the Canon Vitrea Enterprise advanced visualization system at Nuevo Hospital Militar Managua. The solution must ensure business continuity in the event of natural disasters or system failures, with minimal downtime and data loss.

Current State Assessment

  • System: Vitrea Enterprise v7.15.6 deployed on an Enterprise Single Server (ESS) supporting 6 Concurrent Users (CCUs)
  • Storage: The ESS is not connected to external storage (NAS/SAN), meaning all data is stored locally on the server, creating a single point of failure
  • Integration: No integration exists with the existing CMR PACS. Studies are sent directly from modalities to Vitrea
  • Modalities: Multiple high-volume Canon CT, MR, and Angio systems are sending studies to Vitrea
  • Key Limitation: The current ESS-based architecture is inherently not redundant and cannot be easily made redundant without a fundamental upgrade

Recommended Redundancy Solution

The only viable path to true redundancy is to migrate from the ESS to a full Vitrea Enterprise Suite (VES) architecture. The proposed solution involves creating two identical, synchronized datacenters.

Proposed Architecture:

  • Upgrade Path: Convert the existing ESS hardware into a 6-User Application Server
  • Primary & Secondary Datacenters: Each datacenter will contain:
    • 1x Virtualized Management Server: Hosts the core database and services
    • 1x 6-User Application Server: Processes the advanced visualization workload
    • 1x Shared NAS Storage (10 TB+): A centralized storage device accessible by both datacenters
  • Synchronization: The SQL database and NAS storage are continuously synchronized between the two datacenters
  • Failover Mechanism: A DNS CNAME record points users to the active datacenter. In a disaster, this record is updated to redirect all traffic to the secondary site

Recommendation & Next Steps

Approve the upgrade from the ESS-based deployment to a full, redundant VES architecture across two datacenters. This is the only solution that meets the hospital's requirement for resilience against natural disasters.

Project Phasing:

  1. Phase 1 (Foundation): Upgrade the primary site to a full VES with Management Server, Application Server, and connected NAS
  2. Phase 2 (Redundancy): Deploy the identical secondary site infrastructure and establish continuous synchronization

Next Steps:

  1. Executive Approval: Seek approval to present this solution and its budget implications to the customer
  2. Gather Final Details: Confirm from the local team:
    • Formal confirmation on using the existing PACS/VNA for Vitrea storage
    • A finalized budget and timeline from the hospital
    • The exact annual study volume and growth projections
  3. Formal Quote: Generate a detailed quote for the software upgrade, new hardware, and professional services

Apollo Hospitals Vitrea Enterprise Integration

Consolidation of fragmented Vitrea deployment into a unified enterprise solution with seamless PACS integration across multiple Apollo Hospitals sites in India.

  • Challenge
  • Solution
  • Benefits
  • Recommendation

Current Challenges

Apollo Hospitals is facing significant operational inefficiencies and clinical risks due to a fragmented deployment of Canon's Vitrea advanced visualization solution across its sites:

  • Data Silos: Four isolated, unsynchronized databases with different Vitrea versions preventing seamless access to patient images
  • Workflow Disruption: Radiologists must manually search for the same patient in both the PACS and Vitrea systems
  • Version Incompatibility: Post-processed images from older versions cannot be properly restored on newer ones
  • Inefficient Resource Utilization: Vitrea Core licenses are trapped at one site, and high-end workstations are underutilized

Recommended Solution

The proposed solution is to consolidate all existing systems into a single, integrated Vitrea Enterprise Suite (VES) deployment with a centralized database.

Key Components:

  • Centralized Database & Management: A single Management Server hosting the SQL database
  • Hardware Reuse & Upgrade: Strategic repurposing of existing hardware to control costs
  • PACS Integration: Strongly recommended and considered mandatory for project success
  • Centralized Storage: A new 10 TB scalable NAS to serve as the central repository
  • Professional Services: Canon professional services for data migration, deployment, and training

Benefits of the Proposed Solution

  • Seamless Clinical Workflow: Radiologists can access and share patient-specific images from any site
  • Enhanced Patient Safety: Eliminates the risk of opening the wrong patient in Vitrea versus PACS
  • Operational Efficiency: Reduces reporting time and simplifies the radiologist's workflow
  • Vendor Neutrality: The new system can connect to any modality on the network
  • Scalability & Future-Proofing: The enterprise architecture can easily scale by adding more Application Servers
  • Cost Optimization: Maximizes the return on investment from existing hardware and software licenses

Recommendation and Implementation Strategy

Phase 1: Foundation & Consolidation (Priority)

  1. Approve the project to migrate to a single VES deployment
  2. Procure necessary components: NAS storage, GPU upgrades, OS licenses
  3. Deploy the new Management Server, central NAS, and upgraded Application Servers
  4. Implement mandatory PACS integration
  5. Migrate data from old systems to the new central database
  6. Go-Live with the new integrated 6-CCU system

Phase 2: Expansion & Scaling (Future)

  • Add additional CCUs based on demand and user growth
  • Implement a high-availability and disaster recovery solution

Conclusion: Consolidating into a single, integrated Vitrea Enterprise solution with mandatory PACS integration is the only path to achieving the desired seamless workflow, unlocking clinical efficiency, ensuring patient safety, and building a scalable platform for future growth.

Upgrade of Vitrea Enterprise with phased hardware reuse strategy to modernize platform and support future growth at Nawaloka Hospitals in Sri Lanka.

  • Upgrade Strategy
  • Advantages
  • Implementation

Current State & Objective

Nawaloka Hospitals operates a Vitrea Enterprise v6.7 system supporting a limited workflow:

  • Users: 1 Vitrea Advanced & 1 Vitrea Core user
  • Modalities: Connected to a Canon Aquilion ONE CT and a Siemens 3T MRI, with a planned connection to a Philips 1.5T MRI
  • Volume: Handles 35-40 CT and 15-20 MRI studies daily
  • Infrastructure: Uses a 9 TB NAS for storage (VIMS) on a 1 Gbps internal network

Objective: Upgrade to Vitrea v7.14.x to modernize the platform, improve workflow, and build a scalable foundation for future growth, supporting more concurrent users and advanced applications like cardiac MR with Medis.

Recommended Upgrade Strategy

The proposal focuses on cost-effectiveness by maximizing the reuse of existing infrastructure while making strategic new investments.

Proposed Solution Components:

Component Recommendation Rationale
A. Domain Controller Reuse/Upgrade existing or provision a new one (physical/virtual) Required for system integration and user management
B. Management Server Reuse the existing server. Upgrade OS to Windows Server 2019 and SQL to 2019 Cost-saving. The existing hardware is sufficient for the management layer
C. Application Server Procure 1 new Four-User Application Server (e.e., from HP/Dell) This is the core investment. Provides 4 new Advanced CCUs
D. Storage (NAS) Upgrade the existing 9 TB NAS to 16-20 TB Necessary to accommodate increased study volume
E. Software Convert existing Vitrea licenses to v7.14.x. New license for Medis software Unlocks advanced cardiac MR viewing capabilities

Key Advantages of the Proposed Solution

  • Increased Capacity & Performance: Boosts concurrent user capacity from 1 to 4 Advanced users
  • Modernized Platform: Upgrades from the outdated v6.7 to the current v7.14.x
  • Vendor-Neutral Scalability: The new architecture can easily connect to any manufacturer's modality
  • Improved Workflow: Mandatory PACS integration will streamline the radiologist's workflow
  • Future-Proof Foundation: The scalable architecture allows Nawaloka to easily add more CCUs and storage

Implementation & Services

The upgrade requires Canon Professional Services for:

  • PACS Integration: Essential for achieving the desired workflow efficiency
  • Data Migration: Transferring historical data from the old V6.7 system to the new v7.14.x database
  • Deployment & Configuration: Onsite and remote support to ensure a smooth transition and system setup

Conclusion and Recommendation

The recommended phased approach is highly cost-effective, leveraging Nawaloka's existing investment in hardware while strategically upgrading critical components to meet current and future demands.

Approve the project to:

  1. Reuse the existing Management Server and Domain Controller (with OS upgrades)
  2. Procure one new Four-User Application Server
  3. Upgrade the NAS storage to 16-20 TB
  4. Secure licenses for Vitrea v7.14.x and Medis software
  5. Engage Canon Professional Services for integration, migration, and deployment

KPJ Healthcare Advanced Visualization Solution

Deployment of a Vitrea Advanced Visualization (AV) solution across 30 KPJ hospitals with seamless integration to the existing INFINITT PACS-driven workflow.

  • Objective
  • Requirements
  • Architecture
  • Recommendation

Objective

To deploy a Vitrea Advanced Visualization (AV) solution across 30 KPJ hospitals that integrates seamlessly with the existing INFINITT PACS-driven workflow. The solution must enable radiologists to perform complex 3D post-processing and measurements directly from their PACS worklist without disrupting their clinical routine.

Key Functional & Workflow Requirements

  • Seamless PACS Integration: Vitrea must launch via a single click (hyperlink or context menu) from within the INFINITT PACS workstation using Single Sign-On (SSO)
  • Clinical Workflow: The primary reporting template is PACS. Vitrea is used for advanced processing (measurements, key images) with results sent back to PACS as new DICOM series
  • Performance: Must support 60 Concurrent Users (CCUs) across all 30 sites
  • Infrastructure Constraints: Hospitals are connected with limited bandwidth (30-100 MBPS), with distances of ~10km between sites. The existing PACS is confirmed as the primary storage for both thick and thin slice data

Proposed Architectural Options Analysis

Option Architecture Pros Cons
A: Decentralized (30x ESS) An Enterprise Single Server (ESS) deployed at each individual hospital
  • Superior Performance: Processing is local, minimizing latency over low-bandwidth links
  • High Reliability: Failure at one site does not impact others
  • Meets Workflow: Ideal for the required PACS hyperlink/SSO integration
  • Higher initial deployment footprint (30 servers to manage)
B: Centralized (One Enterprise Instance) A single, central server cluster hosting the entire solution for all 30 hospitals
  • Centralized management
  • Potentially lower hardware costs
  • Extreme Performance Risk: 30 MBPS WAN links are insufficient for transferring large volumetric datasets
  • Single Point of Failure: An outage at the central site halts AV for all 30 hospitals
  • Unsuitable for Workflow: Cannot efficiently support the required PACS hyperlink integration

Recommendation and Conclusion

Option A (30 local ESS instances) is the only viable solution given the low network bandwidth and the requirement for a tightly integrated, PACS-driven workflow.

The centralized approach (Option B) is critically flawed due to the network limitations, which would result in an unusable system for radiologists. The decentralized model ensures that image processing happens instantly on the local network, providing the speed and reliability required for clinical diagnostics.

Critical Considerations & Prerequisites for Option A

  • Network: While local performance will be excellent, the 30-100 MBPS links between sites are a critical constraint for syncing processed data (snapshots, reports) to a global PACS. This requires careful planning.
  • Storage: Confirmation that the PACS will store all AV-generated data (both thin and thick slices) is crucial and eliminates the need for a separate large storage solution for Vitrea.
  • Integration: A Domain Controller at each hospital is required for the seamless PACS-AV integration and to enable Auto Query/Retrieve (Auto Q/R), ensuring patient data is available in Vitrea even if not in its local cache.

Next Steps:

  1. Finalize Infrastructure Confirmation: Obtain detailed network diagrams and formal confirmation of PACS storage capabilities for AV data
  2. Plan Deployment: Coordinate the rollout of 30 ESS servers and their integration with the local PACS at each hospital site
  3. Define Sync Strategy: Establish a schedule for syncing processed data from local PACS caches to the global PACS that minimizes impact on the limited WAN bandwidth

Moscow Department of Health Vitrea Enterprise Feasibility

Strategic repurposing of 66 Vitrea Extend workstations into enterprise application servers for cost-effective centralized deployment across the Moscow Department of Health network.

  • Background
  • Objective
  • Solution
  • Considerations

Project Background & Opportunity

The Moscow Department of Health (DoH) is a major healthcare provider that has deployed 66 Canon CT scanners (Aquilion One Genesis, Prime SP, Lightning SP) across its network. Each CT was supplied with a Vitrea Extend advanced visualization workstation (HP Z8G4), creating a fragmented deployment with isolated databases at each site.

This presents a significant opportunity to propose a centralized, enterprise-grade Vitrea solution with minimal additional investment by strategically repurposing this existing hardware.

Customer Need & Strategic Objective

The DoH's centralized AGFA RIS/PACS lacks robust advanced visualization tools. The current fragmented Vitrea Extend deployment creates data silos, preventing seamless access and collaboration.

The key requirement is to create a single, unified database with smart storage and concurrent user licenses, allowing access to advanced tools from any thin client across the hospital network.

Proposed Solution: Cost-Effective Enterprise Deployment

The recommended resolution is an innovative approach to avoid the high cost of new hardware:

  • Repurpose Existing Hardware: Convert the deployed HP Z8G4 Vitrea Extend workstations into 3-user Application Servers for a Vitrea Enterprise deployment
  • Centralized Management: Connect these Application Servers to a central Management Server (which can be virtualized) and a SQL Server
  • Scalable Architecture: A single site with 3 CTs could be configured as:
    • 1x Management Server (with dedicated SQL Server)
    • 1x Domain Controller (preferably provided by the hospital)
    • 1x Scalable NAS (sized for current volume + 20% 5-year growth)
    • 3x Repurposed Z8G4s as Application Servers
    This provides a 9 CCU (Concurrent User) Enterprise solution.

Key Advantages

  • Low Investment: Maximizes value from already-deployed hardware (Z8G4 workstations)
  • Unified Workflow: Replaces isolated databases with a single SQL database, enabling data sharing and collaboration
  • Scalability: The architecture can easily grow by adding more repurposed Z8G4s or standard HPE servers
  • Vendor Neutrality: The enterprise solution can connect to any modality on the network

Key Considerations & Open Questions

The feasibility of this approach depends on resolving several technical questions:

  • Storage: Determining if the Management Server has sufficient internal storage or if an external NAS is required. RAID configuration must be specified
  • Hardware Compatibility: Validating that the Z8G4's existing NVIDIA Quadro P2200 GPU is supported in future Vitrea versions (v7.15.x+)
  • Infrastructure: Confirming power supply adequacy for the Z8G4s in a server role and defining who manages DNS
  • Virtualization: Exploring the option of virtualizing the Management and SQL servers to reduce physical hardware costs and increase flexibility

Conclusion and Next Steps

Repurposing the Vitrea Extend workstations into a centralized Vitrea Enterprise deployment is a highly feasible and strategically advantageous solution for the Moscow DoH. It fulfills their clinical need for a unified workflow at a dramatically lower cost than a traditional new hardware deployment.

Recommended next steps: Finalize technical specifications for hardware, obtain definitive answers on the open questions regarding storage and compatibility, and develop a detailed project plan for a pilot site implementation.

Architectural Solutions Comparison

Different healthcare networks require different architectural approaches based on their specific needs and constraints.

Centralized Architecture

Pros:
  • Centralized database and license management
  • Studies viewable from any hospital
Cons:
  • High network dependency (min. 1GBPS)
  • Potential for image lag

Best for: Networks with excellent connectivity and centralized management requirements.

Decentralized (ESS) Architecture

Pros:
  • Best performance with data processed locally
  • Minimizes latency
Cons:
  • Decentralized database
  • Limited storage per server
  • Less flexible licensing

Best for: Individual hospitals with limited bandwidth between sites.

Hybrid Decentralized Architecture

Pros:
  • Excellent performance for its group
  • Flexible, scalable licensing
Cons:
  • Complex setup
  • Decentralized management
  • Higher initial infrastructure cost

Best for: Hospital groups with good internal connectivity but limited external bandwidth.

Flexible Hybrid Architecture (Recommended)

Pros:
  • Ideal balance of performance without central latency
  • Scalable CCUs
  • Flexible licensing
Cons:
  • Decentralized database requires robust PACS integration

Best for: Multi-hospital networks with geographical distribution and varying bandwidth capabilities.
