Health Imaging Informatics 101

Understanding the terms, technologies, and user stories in medical imaging informatics

Imaging Informatics Terms

Terms commonly used within Imaging Informatics. Many of these terms can be found on sites such as healthinformatics.wikispaces.com.

Electronic Health Records

EMR – Electronic Medical Record

An EMR contains the electronic record of health-related information on an individual. The overall goal of an EMR is to eventually rid practices of using paper medical records.

Not only is the EMR used to document patient care, but the record is also used for financial and legal information, as well as research and quality improvement purposes.

EHR – Electronic Health Record

The EHR is an aggregate electronic record of health-related information on an individual. It is created and gathered cumulatively across more than one healthcare organization and is managed and consulted by licensed clinicians and staff.

By this definition, an EHR is an EMR with integration to other providers' systems.

PHR – Personal Health Record

PHR systems enable patients to maintain their own health records. An ideal PHR would provide a complete and accurate summary of the health and medical history of an individual.

It would accomplish this by gathering data from many sources and making information accessible online to users with the necessary credentials.

Healthcare Systems

HIS – Hospital Information System

The IT applications and systems used to manage hospital operations (e.g., patient financials, registration, scheduling, general financials, back-office systems and order communications).

HIS is a general term encompassing many types of systems, e.g. a RIS is a type of HIS.

HIE – Health Information Exchange

Refers to efforts within regions and communities to integrate various clinical systems such that the information within them can move between the different systems.

With respect to medical imaging, this would enable a provider to access all of a patients studies even if they were acquired and managed at different hospitals in the area.

IDN – Integrated Delivery Network

An IDN is an organization or group of healthcare providers, which, through ownership or formal agreements, aligns local facilities and manages them with one governing board.

IDNs often include hospitals, clinics, imaging centers, nursing homes, and other medical care facilities.

ACO – Accountable Care Organization

An ACO is a provider-based structure in the US, which links reimbursements for care to quality metrics and reductions to the total cost of care.

There are three core principles for ACOs: provider-led organizations, payments linked to quality and cost reduction, and performance metrics.

Imaging Systems

PACS – Picture Archive/Communication System

PACS are systems dedicated to the storage, retrieval, distribution and presentation of images. Typically PACS are turn-key radiology systems that include an archive, radiology diagnostic workstation, radiology work list and enterprise viewer.

A typical PACS network is comprised of a central server that stores a database containing images connected to one or more clients.

RIS – Radiology Information System

These systems became popular for their ability to provide radiology billing services, appointment scheduling as well as reporting and patient database storage.

Today RIS capabilities are often provided by PACS and/or EMR vendors.

VNA – Vendor Neutral Archive

Products that specialize in the archiving of DICOM images and other related data. "Vendor Neutral" refers to the focus on standards compliance and interoperability with all other standards compliant systems.

While PACS sales have primarily been driven by the radiology department, VNA sales are primarily driven by IT in an effort to reduce storage costs.

DICOM – Digital Imaging and Communications in Medicine

The primary standard used in medical imaging. It is designed to ensure the interoperability of various systems by standardizing the way digital imaging is stored, printed, handled and transmitted.

The DICOM standard was first developed in 1985 and renamed DICOM in 1993 with version 3.0.

Universal Viewer

A universal viewer provides general medical image and document access across a diverse medical enterprise environment. Typically launched from EMR, universal viewers connect to multiple data sources to gather and present a consolidated view of a patient.

Because the users are so diverse, the functionality of a universal viewer is kept very simple – aimed towards quick information review in context with an exam report.

ZFC – Zero Footprint Client

ZFC refers to software products that do not require a local install for users to access. True ZFC systems require only HTML and Javascript and run on any web browser.

This delivers a controlled environment and helps ensure service levels can be met, but makes upgrades challenging for vendors.

Order Entry & Decision Support

CPOE – Computerized Physician Order Entry

CPOE allows doctors to electronically prescribe medical orders for their patients. This could be any form of medical orders, i.e. prescriptions, lab tests, referrals, etc.

With proper use CPOE can reduce errors by minimizing the ambiguity of hand-written orders, but a much greater benefit is seen with the combination of CPOE and clinical decision support tools.

DSS – Decision Support System

An interactive computer-based system intended to help decision makers use communications technologies, data, documents, knowledge and/or models to identify and solve problems.

For example: help a physician prescribing a diagnostic test by providing them 'best practices' guidance for the specific disease or symptoms.

Infrastructure & Networking

Virtualization

Virtualization allows a physical resource to appear as one or more logical resources. This can apply to any type of computing resource, e.g. storage, but most often refers to server virtualization.

Server virtualization transparently divides a computer into multiple "virtual" computers. This allows IT to break free from the "one application, one server" rule.

VDI – Virtual Desktop Infrastructure

Virtual desktop infrastructure (VDI) is the practice of hosting a desktop operating system within a virtual machine running on a centralized server.

VDI is a variation on the client/server computing model, sometimes referred to as server-based computing.

SAN – Storage Area Network

A Storage Area Network (SAN) is a networked group of storage devices that can be accessed by multiple computers as if they were directly connected to the computer.

Storage in a SAN is virtualized – logical drives are defined that may cross one or more physical device boundaries.

NAS – Network Attached Storage

Network Attached Storage (NAS) is a group of disks that is network accessible and provides both storage and file system definition.

Computers connect to NAS via file shares over the network. Unlike SAN, the operating system does not see it as locally attached.

Health Information Technology & Informatics

Health Information Technology (HIT)

HIT is a general term encompassing the management of health information through networked computerized systems enabling secure exchange between consumers, providers, government and quality entities, and insurers.

HIT is increasingly viewed as the most promising tool for improving the overall quality, safety and efficiency of the health delivery system. Broad and consistent utilization of HIT will:

  • Improve health care quality
  • Prevent medical errors
  • Reduce health care costs
  • Increase administrative efficiencies
  • Decrease paperwork
  • Expand access to affordable care

Health Imaging Informatics (HII)

Also known as Radiology Informatics or Medical Imaging Informatics, is a subspecialty of Biomedical Informatics that aims to improve the efficiency, accuracy, usability and reliability of medical imaging services within the healthcare enterprise.

This discipline is devoted to the study of how information about and contained within medical images is retrieved, analyzed, enhanced, and exchanged throughout the medical enterprise.

Key Technologies in Imaging Informatics

DICOM

Digital Imaging and Communications in Medicine is the primary standard used in medical imaging. It is designed to ensure the interoperability of various systems by standardizing the way digital imaging is stored, printed, handled and transmitted.

The DICOM standard, initially named "ACR/NEMA 300", was first developed in 1985. It was renamed DICOM in 1993 with version 3.0.

Cloud Computing

Internet-based computing, whereby shared resources, software, and information are provided to computers and other devices on demand, like the electricity grid.

NIST Key characteristics of cloud: On-demand self-service, broad network access, resource pooling, location independence, rapid elasticity, and measured service.

Virtualization

Allows a physical resource to appear as one or more logical resources. This can apply to any type of computing resource, e.g. storage, but most often refers to server virtualization.

Server virtualization transparently divides a computer into multiple "virtual" computers. This allows IT to break free from the "one application, one server" rule.

Universal Viewer

Provides general medical image and document access across a diverse medical enterprise environment. Typically launched from EMR, universal viewers connect to multiple data sources to gather and present a consolidated view of a patient.

Because the users are so diverse, the functionality of a universal viewer is kept very simple – aimed towards quick information review in context with an exam report.

Context Launching

Refers to the integration between two applications such that the context is passed from the launching application (such as a PACS) to the launched application (such as Vitrea).

Context typically includes user name, patient demographics and study identifiers. This saves users time so they don't have to log in to the launched application and search for the patient/study.

Software Rendering

Advanced computer graphics applications, such as 3D medical imaging, traditionally require a powerful graphics card (GPU). Recent improvements in CPU processing power make it possible for applications to handle advanced graphics processing without a GPU.

This CPU-based rendering is also referred to as software rendering.
